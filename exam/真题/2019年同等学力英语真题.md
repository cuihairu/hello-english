### Part I Oral Communication（15 minutes, 10 points）

#### Section A

**Dialogue One**

A. They are due today.
B. That sounds like a good idea.
C. Got any plans after the class?

Juliane: Hi, Jenny. **(1) C**

Jenny: Not really. What about you?

Juliane: I've got a course paper to write. So I plan to look for some books and study at the library. Do you want to join me?

Jenny: Sure. I need to return some books. **(2) A** Besides, I can also read some magazines and newspapers there.

Juliane: Fantastic! We can go swimming in the gym after that.

Jenny: **(3) B** We can chill out a little in such a hot summer.

**答案：(1) C (2) A (3) B**

**Dialogue Two**

A. Let's read this book.
B. You've been saying that forever!
C. There must be ten here.

Girl: Dad, will you read to me?

Dad: Uh, let me finish the newspaper first?

Girl: **(4) B**

Dad: Well, how about reading the business section of the newspaper together?

Girl: That's boring. **(5) A** It's about a bear and a cat that become friends. And these books too.

Dad: Whoa. I thought you said one book. **(6) C**

Girl: My teacher, Mrs Green, says you have to read to me every night, and the newspaper doesn't count.

**答案：(4) B (5) A (6) C**

#### Section B

**Dialogue Three**

A. What is your name?
B. but that class is already full.
C. but the computer won't let me.
D. You're a Comp Lit major?

Stuart: Hello. Can I help you?

Karen: Yes, I hope so. I would like to register for Comp Lit 287.

Stuart: I'm sorry, **(7) B** And also, students are supposed to register through the touch-tone registration system.

Karen: I know. I already tried to register for it by phone, **(8) C**

Stuart: That's because it's full.

Karen: But I'm a new student here. I thought maybe there was some way I could get into the class.

Stuart: Well, I could put you on a waiting list. But that doesn't guarantee you will get into the class. **(9) A**

Karen: My name is Karen Huang.

Stuart: Alright. And what is your major?

Karen: I'm a comparative literature major.

Stuart: Wait a minute. **(10) D**

Karen: Yes, that's right. And Professor Cohen told me I need to take Comp Lit 287.

Stuart: The university computer system saves extra places, in class 287 for Comp Lit students.

Karen: Even if the class is full?

Stuart: That's right.

**答案：(7) B (8) C (9) A (10) D**

**Passage One**

As 170 people sat down to dinner, I breathed a sigh of relief: The conference was going well. Running it was part of my job as a university program manager, working on a project to boost biotech collaborations between academia and industry. When I started in the role a few years earlier, I thought that maybe, after years of career exploration, I had finally found the right job for me. But at the conference, I found myself wondering whether that was really what I wanted from my career. I'm a scientist, not an event planner—but I had been too busy organizing the conference to appreciate the research being discussed. Was it time for yet another change?

I first started looking for jobs when I was finishing my PhD in 2012, applying for tenure-track positions. The process was extremely competitive. After several interviews, I failed to land a faculty job. I was burnt out from applying for academic positions, and looking for something different to do with my life.

I found a position as a science policy fellow at a professional scientific society. Over the next few years, I moved between jobs in science policy, communications, and administration. I enjoyed aspects of each of them, but I never felt fully satisfied.

Then, I heard about the university program manager position. It seemed perfect—I could work at the interface of science and business, facilitating connections between researchers and companies. I applied and got the job. I was excited to be working in a role that used my scientific background while also involving project management and communication.

But sitting at that conference dinner, I realized something important. I had spent the entire day dealing with logistics—the caterer was late, the projector broke, and I had to calm a speaker who was unhappy with the room arrangement. I hadn't had a single meaningful conversation about the science being presented. I missed being a researcher.

It took me several more months to come to terms with this realization. I had invested so much time and energy into building a career outside academia that it was difficult to admit it wasn't working. But eventually, I decided to make a change. I started applying for research positions again, and after a few months, I accepted a postdoctoral research position at a government laboratory.

The transition wasn't easy. I had to rebuild my research skills and catch up on the latest developments in my field. But I was finally doing what I loved—conducting experiments, analyzing data, and contributing to scientific knowledge. I had come full circle, but the journey had taught me valuable lessons about myself and what I needed from my career.

## Paper Two（试卷二）

### Part VI Translation（10 points）

Directions: Translate the following passage into Chinese. Write your answer on the Answer Sheet.

In life, once on a path, we tend to follow it, for better or worse. Few of us ask ourselves if we are heading in the right direction. Instead, we compare ourselves to those around us—our friends, colleagues, and neighbors. We use their achievements and lifestyles as benchmarks for our own progress. But this comparison can be misleading. What works for one person may not work for another. Each of us has unique talents, values, and circumstances that shape what a successful life looks like.

The problem with following the crowd is that we may end up pursuing goals that don't truly matter to us. We may climb the corporate ladder only to find that the view from the top doesn't inspire us. We may accumulate wealth only to realize that money doesn't bring lasting happiness. The key is to define success on our own terms, based on our own values and aspirations.
